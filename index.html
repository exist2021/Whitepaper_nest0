1.5 Instructive History: The Demise of Gold as Money

A timeline illustrating the centralization of money through the elimination of gold:

Circa 1910

Travelers carried British gold sovereigns for international use (Bombay, Shanghai, Sydney).

1914

Most gold coins held by the public were surrendered to banks for paper notes to finance WW1.

1919-1933

Central Banks required Commercial Banks to surrender gold bullion for reserve account credits. Major countries devalued currencies relative to gold; some made it illegal for citizens to hold physical gold.

1944

Bretton Woods agreements fixed gold at $35/ounce, mandating it only for international balance of payments settlements.

1968

Requirement for Federal Reserve notes to be covered by 25% physical gold was eliminated.

August 15, 1971

President Richard Nixon suspended the redemption of dollars held by foreign trading partners for physical gold.

By 1974

All major currency issuers moved from fixed to floating exchange rates.

August 14, 1974

President Gerald Ford signed a law allowing Americans to own gold coins and bullion again, but gold was no longer currency.

1974-2008

Financialization created new instruments (Stocks, Bonds, Derivatives) as better Stores of Value and Units of Account, expanding money supply exponentially.

Post-2018

Cryptocurrencies emerge as new entrants, representing collective optimism rather than underlying financial assets.

This history shows money evolving from tangible to symbolic, reflecting a "super-symbolic" Digital economy where wealth is based on progressive derivation.

▼

2.1 Core Technologies of Web3 Level 0

Web3 Level0 is built on two core technologies:

  • DHT (Distributed Hash Table): Enables decentralization by having network participants provide computing and storage.
  • Cryptographically Secure Hash Chains: Builds trust by providing verifiable and tamper-proof data shared among participants.

This design allows for integrating real-world information (e.g., Passport, Aadhar) into hash chains for unique participant identification, balancing decentralization with adherence to national laws.

2.2 Network Architecture

  • Participants act as nodes in the DHT, running the application and providing storage.
  • Each participant stores their own data and randomly selected data from peers, all within locally stored, tamper-proof cryptographically secured hashchains.
  • Data access is facilitated through a gossip protocol among peers.
  • Key Distinction: Unlike Bitcoin, there is no single chain. Data redundancy is maintained as per network requirements, and consensus is left to application logic.
  • Scalability: The average time complexity handled by each participant decreases as the number of participants in the network grows.

$$ \text{Participating Node} \in \frac{(\log(m) + v(n, m))}{m} $$

Where:

  • $n=$ total number of public transactions in the system
  • $m=$ total number of participants
  • $\log(m)$ is the complexity of finding the participant storing the data
  • $v(n,m)$ is the complexity for validating a specific entry (depends on system requirements, can grow exponentially if multiple hops for past transactions are needed)

2.3 Data Integrity and Resilience

Ensuring data reliability and availability:

  • Data Integrity: Achieved through participant validation using cryptography and peer witnessing.
    • Cryptographic Hashes: Participants locally detect tampered data by comparing hashes; public key cryptography proves authorship.
    • Peer Witnessing: Data is witnessed, validated, and stored by a random selection of participants. Cooperating participants detect invalid data, spread evidence of corrupt participants, and counteract threats.
  • Data Resilience: Maintained based on system uptime requirements, ensuring data availability even if some nodes are unavailable.

$$ \text{uptime}(n) \ge r $$

Where:

  • uptime(n) $\in [0, 1]$
  • $r$ is the required resiliency
  • The number of nodes $q$ storing data for a node must be chosen to meet the total uptime for resiliency requirements.

2.4 Incentive Model: Participation as Reward

The core incentive model:

Simple Incentive:

Each participant runs the complete application and provides storage for their own data, plus a small set of data from other participants. The incentive is simply the ability to participate in the network.

2.5 Proof of X: Bridging Digital and Real Worlds

The network provides mechanisms to bridge digital and real-world identities and trust:

  • Proof of Presence: Applications can mandate real-world identities (e.g., Passport, Aadhar) as a requirement for participation.
  • Proof of Stake (Trust): Applications can require invites from known individuals, building on real-world connections and trust ("staking the trust built in the real world!").

2.6 Data Ownership: Decentralized and Adaptive

How data ownership is handled in the system:

  • Public data is stored with transaction participants and randomly selected peers.
  • The legality of data ownership is left to the system's evolving framework.
  • Future enhancements will allow for consensus-based sharing of data outside the network.
